Wiring & Installation Guidance

Cable Issues: Why DC Solar Cable Matters

Why It Matters: Many installers use 2×6mm² normal household cable from panels to inverter. While this works, it is not efficient and reduces your system's performance over time.

Good to Know: Regular 2×6 cable works but is not designed for outdoor DC solar use. Over time, sun exposure breaks down the insulation and resistance causes power loss.

Why Upgrading Makes a Difference:

  • Power loss from resistance — up to 15–20% of your solar energy wasted as heat
  • Insulation degrades in sun — regular cable isn't UV-rated, cracks after a few years
  • Higher resistance = more heat buildup in cables, especially on hot days
  • Warranty considerations — some manufacturers require proper solar cable
  • Long-term reliability — solar cable lasts 25+ years vs regular cable degrading in 3–5 years in sun

The Right Cable: PV1-F / H1Z2Z2-K DC Solar Cable

  • UV-resistant outer sheath — survives 15+ years in direct sun
  • Rated for 1500VDC — safe for panel voltages up to 1000VDC
  • Double-insulated — two layers of protection
  • Temperature range: -40°C to +90°C — works in extreme climates
  • Low resistance — 80mΩ/m ensures minimal power loss
  • MC4 connectors — industry standard, reliable contact

Our Recommendation: When you can, upgrade to certified solar DC cable (PV1-F rated) with MC4 connectors. It costs slightly more but saves you 15–20% of your solar power and lasts for the lifetime of your system.

Battery Ventilation — Critical for Safety

Lead-Acid Batteries (AGM) produce hydrogen gas during charging. Enclosed spaces = explosion risk.

Danger: Hydrogen gas is explosive. No sparks, flames, or cigarettes within 3 meters of battery.

Minimum Requirements:

  • Ventilated room — windows or vents allowing airflow
  • No sparks/flames — keep away from welding, grinding, smoking
  • Raised off floor — hydrogen rises; floor level is safest but air must circulate
  • Temperature control — 15–25°C optimal; >45°C reduces lifespan
  • No direct sunlight — causes heat and accelerates aging

Lithium Batteries don't produce hydrogen but need cooling airflow to prevent thermal issues and maintain optimal performance.

Full System Wiring Diagram

Correct wiring sequence:

  1. Solar panels → DC solar cable (PV1-F) → MC4 connectors
  2. MC4 connectors → Inverter PV input (respect polarity)
  3. Battery terminals → Proper gauge cable (see table below)
  4. Battery cable → Inverter battery input
  5. Inverter AC output → Main distribution board/breaker
  6. Distribution board → Appliances

Essential Safety Components:

  • Circuit breaker/fuse on battery side — typically 100A for 48V systems
  • Circuit breaker/fuse on AC output side — protects inverter and grid
  • Earth/ground connection — MUST be present for personal safety
  • DC disconnect switch — allows safe maintenance

Panel Cleaning Guide

Why Cleaning Your Panels Matters

Dust, sand, bird droppings, and pollen build up on your panels every day. Even a thin layer of dust can block 15–25% of sunlight from reaching the cells. That means your system produces less power, your batteries charge slower, and you run out of energy sooner. Regular cleaning is the easiest and cheapest way to get more from the system you already have.

How to Clean Your Panels

  1. When to clean: Early morning or late afternoon when panels are cool. Never clean hot panels in midday sun — cold water on hot glass can crack it.
  2. What you need: A bucket of clean water, a soft cloth or sponge, and optionally a soft-bristle brush with a long handle for rooftop panels.
  3. Step 1: Rinse the panel surface with water to remove loose dust and sand. Do not scrub dry panels — dry dust scratches the glass.
  4. Step 2: Gently wipe with a wet soft cloth or sponge. For stubborn spots (bird droppings, tree sap), let a wet cloth sit on the spot for a few minutes to soften it, then wipe.
  5. Step 3: Rinse again with clean water and let air dry.

What NOT to Do

  • No soap or detergent — it leaves a film that attracts more dust and reduces output
  • No abrasive materials — steel wool, rough sponges, or dry rags will scratch the glass permanently
  • No high-pressure water — it can damage seals and push water into the frame or junction box
  • Never stand on panels — they will crack, even if they look strong

How Often to Clean

  • Dusty or sandy areas: Every 1–2 weeks
  • Urban or less dusty areas: Once a month
  • After a dust storm or heavy winds: Clean as soon as possible
  • After rainy season: Rain helps but often leaves mud streaks — clean once rain stops

A good habit: Check your panels every time you check your battery voltage. If they look dusty, clean them. 10 minutes of cleaning can give you 20% more energy that day.

Rainy Season Advice

Managing Your System During Rainy Season

During the rainy season, cloud cover reduces solar panel output significantly — sometimes by 50–70%. Your system will generate less energy, so you need to adjust your usage to avoid draining your batteries too fast.

Reduce Your Energy Usage

  • Postpone heavy appliances — avoid running the iron, water pump, or washing machine on cloudy days. Wait for a sunny day or run them only when you see the sun is out.
  • Use only essential appliances — lights, phone charging, and fridge. Everything else can wait.
  • Turn off standby devices — TVs, decoders, and chargers left plugged in use power even when off.
  • Limit nighttime usage — if batteries didn't fully charge during the day, use as little as possible at night.

Protect Your Equipment

  • Check all cable connections — rain and moisture can cause corrosion on exposed terminals. Inspect MC4 connectors and battery terminals.
  • Keep water away from inverter and batteries — if your equipment room leaks, fix it before the rains come. Water and electricity are a deadly combination.
  • Check roof mounting — strong winds during storms can loosen panel brackets. Tighten bolts and check frame integrity.
  • Ventilation still matters — don't seal the battery room shut to keep rain out. Batteries still need airflow. Use rain covers, not sealed doors.

After the Rains

  • Clean your panels — rain washes away some dust but leaves mud streaks and water marks. Clean panels properly once the rain stops.
  • Inspect for rust or corrosion — check all metal parts, brackets, and cable connectors.
  • Fully charge your batteries — after a period of low charging, give your batteries a full charge on the first sunny day. This is especially important for AGM batteries to prevent sulfation.
